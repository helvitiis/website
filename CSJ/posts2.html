Q&A: Advice from an Indie Comic Artist

Allyson Hall | 11 Feb 2018

In 2016, comics and graphic novel sales in the US and Canada totalled $1.085 billion--a 5% increase from the previous year--and have been rising steadily since 2011 according to Comichron, the largest public repository of comic book sales figures. Rising figures are due largely in part to online vendors such as Comixology (owned by Amazon), Graphic.ly, and DriveThru Comics--DriveThru Comics even offers the ability of printing digital comics through their store.

Independent media has steadily been on the rise with the advancement of technology and the freedom the internet and networking provides. With the ability to publish and read comics online in a digital format, this allows artists and writers a greater window of opportunity for presenting their creative works to the world.

Tom Rogers is a freelance comic and storyboard artist based in Oregon who, between juggling employment for the art of the Marvel TL;DR YouTube series, the gaming industry, and Patreon commissions, works on independent comics for a living. I asked him for an interview by email to answer some questions about the independent comics industry and popular comic Herald, which is now published by Action Lab Entertainment.

What first drew you into creating comics as a career?

I've been making comics for fun (and for my friends and family) for as long as I can remember. It wasn't until my high school art teacher, Mr. Sutton, specifically told me I could and should do this for a living that I began to seriously consider turning my hobby into a career. I also came to the realization that drawing is the thing I did best (and loved doing the most) so I had to give it a shot!

Are there any comics you loved when you were younger? How might you have been influenced by them?

My favorite comics as a kid were Spider-Man, Sonic the Hedgehog, Star Wars, BONE, and I'm not sure if this counts fully as a comic, but also the Captain Underpants series. My two favorite newspaper comic strips were Calvin & Hobbes and Peanuts. All of these comics still hold a special place in my heart and have influenced me in so many ways. While I never copied an artist's drawings directly, the artwork in all of these comics subconsciously affected how I draw and see the world. Probably the most clear artistic influence on my own work is Jeff Smith's BONE series. Beyond that, I have comics to thank for my love of reading as well as my moral code (thanks to Peter Parker).

What is your favourite part of working on Herald? Is there a downside to working on the comic?

My favorite part of working on Herald is the collaboration with my creative team. I eagerly anticipate each new script from author John Reilly, who has crafted a unique character-driven sci-fi/fantasy epic. I love the writing for every character in the series -- my absolute favorite being Thomas Edison's (fictional) slimey lawyer, Mackleroy. John often asks me to draw very ambitious scenes, which I'll have to push myself as an artist to achieve. (Artists truly experience growth when we stray away from our comfort zones.) Dexter Weeks's work as colorist and letterer is some of the best in the biz, further enhancing the artwork and storytelling. I'm a big fan of his eye-poppin' color palettes which have definitely influenced my own solo color work.  The downside to working on the series is that, as a royalties-only indie comic, it doesn't make us creators much of an income (certainly not a reliable one). I've been able to earn a living as an artist through my other freelance work (recently, concept art for a mobile video game and storyboard illustrations for Marvel's YouTube series, Marvel: TL;DR, as well as an art assistant for more well-established artists) in addition to the support from wonderful people on my Patreon webpage (shameless plug: patreon.com/tomrogerscomics)!

What is your process on working each panel to completion, and about how long does it take for you to finish a page?

My process for drawing a comic page begins with 2 x 3" thumbnail sketches in which I make all the major visual storytelling decisions at a small scale. Next, I scale up the thumbnail sketches and print them out on 11 x 17" bristol board in non-photo blue ink and begin the pencil stage, doing my best to perfect the anatomy and perspective in each panel. If I'm having trouble drawing a particular character pose, I'll often ask a friend to pose for me and snap some pics for reference to improve the drawing. After that, I clean up the line-art either by inking over it by hand (I'm mostly a brush and ink kinda guy) or tightening up the pencils a bit digitally in Clip Studio Paint using my trusty Wacom tablet. On average, a single black and white comic page takes me a full workday (8-12 hours depending on the page's complexity). A single panel can take anywhere from 30 minutes to 4 hours (for a complicated establishing shot).

Back in 2015, Herald was proposed as a television series to be developed though Romark Entertainment. Is this still an ongoing process?

While the TV option with Romark has just recently expired, I still hold some hope for Herald's potential to be adapted to TV or film by another production company somewhere down the line. As my writer John Reilly puts it, getting an option deal is like buying a lottery ticket. Even once a production company has acquired the rights, chances are pretty slim that all the pieces of the puzzle will fall into place. The comic series is always my primary focus, but it would be pretty sweet to see Herald come to life in live action (or animated)!

Herald started out as an indie comic written by John Reilly, with you as the penciller and Dexter Weeks as the colourist and letterer, before Action Lab Entertainment became your publisher. How different is it working on an indie comic versus publishing through Action Lab?

The biggest difference between self-publishing and publishing under Action Lab is their connections to comics news sites and Diamond, the largest international printer and distributor of comics. And of course, now we have some editorial involvement, which, I'm thankful to say, has been for the betterment of the series.

Do you have any advice for college students that might want to break into the comic business or indie / web comic scene?

A. My primary piece of advice is the most simple: draw every day! Even when you're not in the mood, crack open that sketchbook and put pencil, pen, paintbrush, or somethin' to paper! Draw from life, draw from photos, draw from your own imagination, draw from anatomy books, practice perspective drawing, but most importantly just keep drawing! Got a great idea for a web comic on your brain? To quote Shia LaBeouf, "Just do it!" (He actually did make a comic come to think of it...)

B. More specific advice is to know the ending and plan out the major bullet-points of the plot. Even if your comic meanders a bit along the way, knowing the ending will give you a solid goal to shoot for. There are so many web comics that have been abandoned simply because the creators lost the drive to finish them and/or didn't know how to finish them.

C. Make it short! While you may want your first comic outing to be a grand space opera, your magnum opus, if you can actually FINISH it, you can feel proud of your completed comic (which is easier to present to comic professionals). I would recommend making a 5-10 page comic at first and shooting for loftier lengths as you continue to complete more ambitious projects. As previously mentioned, a lot of comics get abandoned for one reason or another. Beyond not knowing the ending, many comics are abandoned due to a shift in skill level. Once an artist feels they have "one-upped" their drawing XP (am I combining video game metaphors?), they will feel the urge to either tear up all their older work or redraw it completely. This can become an ENDLESS cycle that you don't want to get sucked into. When a page is done, it's done. Don't look back! Done is beautiful!

D. I highly recommend the following how-to books:

  • How to Draw Comics the Marvel Way by Stan Lee and John Buscema
  • Understanding Comics and Making Comics by Scott McCloud
  • Figure Drawing For All It's Worth by Andrew Loomis
  • Vanishing Point: Perspective for Comics by Jason Cheeseman-Meyer

E. There's much more advice I would love to impart to my younger self (and any aspiring comic artists), but I don't want to overwhelm your readers here. Feel free to have them shoot me specific questions to my email tomrogersart@gmail.com and I'll do my best to give them the lowdown!


HEART OF GOLD

An Indie Web Comic

Allyson Hall | 2 Feb 2018

Heart of Gold is an LGBT+ indie web comic that began back in February of 2017. It's the brainchild of freelance artists Eli Baumgartner and Viv Tanner who reside in Vienna, Austria. Since publishing their comic on the web, they have been offered a position to publish through Sparkler, a popular monthly digital magazine that distributes comics, prose, and audio for free.

The gist of the comic follows main characters Dunant, a priest, and Ionel, a career pianist. Ionel suffers from a condition known as Nystagmus, making it nearly impossible to play the piano as his vision is so badly blurred. The priest Dunant regularly holds 'faith healing' masses that Ionel consistently attends, hoping to be healed. However, Ionel finds that something isn't right about Dunant's blessings, and decides to investigate the matter.

The comic features breathtaking art in selective color palettes and promises an aesthetically pleasing read blended with a story based on mystery and suspense. The style is reminiscent of paint on a canvas, rather than the traditional 'comic book style' many comics still possess today.

Eli is the main artist and co-writer of the comic, while Viv applies the colors and lettering. The comic has 184 patrons on Patreon, and you can support them by visiting https://www.patreon.com/heartofgoldcomic. You can read the comic online at https://sparklermonthly.com/series/heart-of-gold/.
