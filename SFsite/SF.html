SCIENCE FICTION RESEARCH AND CREATIVE ADAPTATION

Science fiction is "a form of fiction that draws imaginatively on scientific knowledge and speculation in its plot, setting, theme, etc." according to its official definition on Dictionary.com. While this is true, science fiction is comprised of many different sub-genres and brings hundreds of different facets to the table. Writers such as H.G. Wells with War of the Worlds and Octavia Butler with Lilith's Brood deal with the technology of aliens and play with alternate timelines. It can seem like cosmic horror and space are often the running veins of many science fiction novels, books, and other types of media, but there are also many exceptions to this line of thought. Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? by Philip K. Dick briefly mentions colonies on Mars, but the central focus of the plot is on Earth, while Watchmen by Alan Moore has a similar focus (with brief mentions of Dr. Manhattan on Mars. Mars is a popular planet in science fiction). The Handmaid's Tale by Margaret Atwood has hardly any mention if at all of technological advancement--Offred's story is recorded on cassette tapes. Though not all science fiction pieces deal with technology, many do--including 2001. Gary Leonard puts this simply:

Although Science Fiction extensively features various products of technology, and might fairly be said to show an obsession with gadgets, it is not really the buttons, dials, and gizmos that drive this genre. What seems of more interest is the way these things reflect a way of thinking, and a method for ordering and organizing the world around us, that then serves as the ground permitting us to perceive humanity as moving in a direction we characterize as moving "forward." The perception of this direction as "forward," thereby constituting "progress," is dependent on seeing the gadget as a "new" invention predicated on a "discovery." Discovery and progress are conflated into a general notion of "progress".[0]

Hal is a machine. It's clear that Arthur C. Clarke intended for Hal to be something more than this though, as he inadvertently breaks Isaac Asimov's three laws of robotics to prove a point in the novel (perhaps at no fault of his own): 1) A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm; 2) A robot must obey orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law; and, 3) A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law. [1] Though it's easy to say Hal followed the third law, the first and second are broken as Hal makes the choice not to save Poole in Chapter 25 of 2001.[2] It's also fair to say Hal doesn't qualify as a robot because of his heightened intelligence. This may be a valid statement, but some points can be argued that Hal is, indeed, qualified to be labelled as a robot--more specifically an artificially intelligent robot. Hal is able to be programmed, yet is also able to learn on his own via particular algorithms.[3] From Nathan Abrams' article entitled What was HAL? IBM, Jewishness and Stanley Kubrick's 2001: A Space Odyssey,

"HAL has been described as more human than any of the astronauts in the film. HAL is, in theory, capable of emotional responses indistinguishable from those displayed by his human counterparts but many have commented that HAL appears more rounded and sympathetic than they do. For example, when Poole receives birthday greetings from his distant parents on Earth, he appears to be bored. Furthermore, we experience events from HAL's subjective point of view; in fact, we are even invited inside HAL's mind and memory, learning of his 'childhood' and 'birth', as well as physically entering his 'brain'. As a consequence of this personalization and individuation, 'the audience feels sympathy for HAL when he is finally disconnected. None of the other characters in the film is ever presented with this degree of empathy'."[4]

Abrams agrees that Hal must be more than a machine, which makes sense in the context Clarke presents.

In the first few sentences in Chapter 3 of 2010: odyssey two, Dr. Chandra is introduced as being so passionate about Hal that, as he learns of the deaths of the Discovery crew, he "griev[es] only for his lost child, HAL 9000." Dr. Chandra's unremitting reverence toward Hal during his development and learning period may be a contributing factor to Hal's aberrent behavior which puzzles everyone (including his creator). Abrams helps to support this claim that Hal must have a connection to humans harbored, perhaps originally, by Dr. Chandra. "...when HAL refers to Bowman as 'Dave' rather than 'David'," Abrams writes, "this has the effect of indicating friendship and familiarity, even intimacy, between man and computer..."

When I went about deriving a seperate work of fiction from Kubrick and Clarke's alternate world, I wanted to make certain I was able to understand the characters and their motivations, which I knew would be difficult in the format I chose to present the adaptation. It's not easy to show how a character feels by only revealing their words, but I felt it would be crucial to reveal the situation from Hal's point of view. He recieves raw data and must learn to sift through it, draw out the crucial points that he can build on, and learn from his mistakes or successes. Here, I'm forcing the reader to deal with the same kind of blinders Hal has, or for that regard, any self-aware object equipped with the technology of intelligence and machine learning today.

Clarke's background as a trained physicist and mathematician allowed him a mindset to delve into "hard science fiction," characterized by precise and scientifically accurate themes and world building.[5][6] This should be observed when attempting an adaptation from his work, which I faithfully undertook; part of my future career path involves scientific writing on technology, and so with the credentials I hold in that regard, I set to work expanding on Hal's character and motivation. He is seemingly proud of himself and holds himself to a high standard, and as an intelligent machine is able to process information in a way that gives him the illusion of being a living being. There are ethics and debates that aren't necessary to get into now, but for all purposes in 2001, Hal thinks and chooses his responses to situations based on facts and his given input. It's also important to note that Hal is given a gender and referred to as "he" or "him," which personifies him from simply "an AI."

On a stylistic side note, I designed the adaptation page to resemble a CTR monitor similar to those used in the late seventies onward until the 2000s, and I crafted the text to look like a data sheet that could have been pulled from Hal's memory banks. A lot of it will seem a little odd or out of place--this is because Hal's memory banks have already been removed by Dave at this point, and we are taking Hal's memories at face value. It is noted in 2001 that Hal's memories may have been wiped all but for a few, and so I used this idea to build upon for my project.
